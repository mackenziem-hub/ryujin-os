A full breakdown of the two Charlotte Street metal proposals under the locked three-package structure: Standard ribbed, European Clay, and Premium snap-lock. What each sells for, what it costs to build, the gross margin, and the real net profit after overhead, sales and marketing. Numbers run on the divisor method from the 2026 Metal SOP.

The model

How the price is built.

Three real packages, not a finish toggle. Each prices off a divisor against hard cost. The divisor sets the gross margin, and the better-protected build earns the higher one.

Where the margin goes

Gross margin to real profit.

Gross margin is not profit. Out of it comes overhead (20%), sales (10%) and marketing (5%) of the sale. What is left is net profit in your pocket, and it climbs with the tier.

The labour reality

Why Premium jumps.

Metal labour is not flat. Base install runs $290/sq, but a steep 12/12 carries a pitch premium, and the Premium package stacks a full tear-off and a brand-new deck on top of that base.

The read

What this tells us.

  • All three packages hold a healthy 47 to 52% gross margin by design. The divisor method is doing its job.
  • European Clay is the smart middle sell: a real step up in look and protection, ~$4.7k net on Catherine, and it earns the 25-year warranty.
  • Premium carries the richest net (~$8k Catherine, ~$5.7k Desiree) because the divisor headroom widens on the most-protected build, even with the new deck eating cost.
  • Two flags to lock: the snap-lock panel supply quote (Premium estimate) and Desiree's pitch confirmation. Lock those and every number here is exact.
Structure locked this round: standing seam is out (we have never installed it), Premium is now snap-lock interlock, and clay is its own mid-tier package, not a cheaper finish on the same build.
