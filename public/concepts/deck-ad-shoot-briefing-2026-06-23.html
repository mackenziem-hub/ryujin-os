The delivery north star

The whole delivery fix is one switch: talk to one homeowner, not a camera.

Pick a real person before each take, a Karla, a Wendy, and tell them what you would tell a neighbor across the truck hood. When your brain is on the person, the read is confident. When it is on the words, it goes stiff.

Clean-shaven, shoulders back and down, chin level, feet planted. The fresh look plus controlled stillness is the trust signal that closes a roofing lead.

What separates a confident read from a stiff one

Three habits carry every take. Drill these, ignore the rest.

01

Talk to one homeowner. The mental switch above. Confidence comes from being on the person, not the script.

02

Slower than feels right, silence after every number. A beat of silence after "$3,340" reads as confidence. Talking over it reads as nerves.

03

Lock the lens on the hook and the CTA. Those are your two eye-contact moments. Drift slightly off-lens while you teach, snap back to close. Never swallow the CTA.

Shoot order · the hook is the lever

Film every hook first. That is the test that wins.

Five hooks per offer, each recorded 2 to 3 times with the energy deliberately varied, one steady, one warmer, one with a touch more punch. The hook is the single biggest lever on a paid ad, so give the edit a real choice and let the funnel kill the losers fast.

Start each take already "on," do not ease in. The first line competes with a thumb, so it gets full lens contact and a hair more presence than the rest.

The beats that carry each ad

Three money moments. Slow down and let them land.

Revive, the crack then the flex. Say the line, go silent, bend the old shingle slow until it cracks. Then swap and spring the flexible one on the word "flex." This is the single most important timing in the batch.

10CM, the 80% stat. "The biggest one?" real pause. "Eighty percent of failures are the install, not the shingle," slow right down and let it sit. That number is the whole clip.

Revive, the price reveal. "Sixteen thousand five" let it sit, "thirty-three forty" say it almost easy. The gap between the two numbers is where the viewer does the math. That is the sell.

Why this shoot moves the real number

Every clip drives the tracked estimator, so it moves cost-per-booked, not just CPL.

Meta books an inspection at $602 today. The pixel CPL says $26, but that is the price of a price-reveal, not a customer. These ads exist to bend the $602 down, and because each clip points at the tracked estimator with its own utm, the funnel will show you which hook books cheapest.

You can finally see which ad books a roof. So shoot wide and let the data pick.

01

The ad is not the product, the booked inspection is. Film for the homeowner who books, not the one who claps. Every line in the sheet sells the outcome, the 60-second number, never the process.

02

Shoot many hooks on purpose. You used to guess which creative worked. Now 15 hooks go up paused, the funnel scores each one's cost-per-booked, and the winner compounds while the losers die in a week.

03

The clean read is the close. Calm, slow, honest, one homeowner in your eye. The "I'll tell you straight if yours isn't a fit" line, delivered dead into the lens, is worth more than any hook on the list.
