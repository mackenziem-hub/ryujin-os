How work moves

Work flows one way: order in, result out, OneDrive carries it between machines

Nobody types into worker terminals. Orders are files, results are files, and OneDrive is the wire connecting both PCs and your phone.

Every desk has an inbox (sessions/B-inbox.json). Anything that writes a valid order into it puts that desk to work on its next cycle.

  1. Order created by you (dispatch form, phone) or by the foreman (auto-generated from the sprint queue).
  2. Desk pulls it on its /desk loop: claims the top priority, sets its status to working.
  3. Executes under guardrails: workers never deploy, never send outbound, never touch prod data directly.
  4. Result written back as one line: shipped, READY FOR DEPLOY, BLOCKED ON MAC, or RATE LIMITED.
  5. Wall + foreman read it and the next order goes out. The cycle compounds.

Your controls

You run it with three controls, none of them a terminal

Dispatch

SEND ORDER form on the wall or mobile.html on your phone. Pick a desk, type the ask, done. Your P0 outranks anything the foreman queued.

The gold "!"

The only signal that needs you NOW. A desk hit something only Mac can decide. Everything else keeps itself busy.

The merge window

Workers stop at PR open. Once a day you review the stack, Terminal A batch-merges and deploys. Your judgment is the only gate to prod.

Hard lines that never move

No worker deploys. No terminal sends anything outbound, ever, regardless of permission mode. Customer comms stay draft-only with your sign-off. Prod data writes route through Terminal A only.

From the road

Same screens over Tailscale once installed (10 minutes, both PCs + phone). Chrome Remote Desktop for the rare case a terminal needs your hands.

08 / 08
Operator read

You built a labor multiplier; your review is now the bottleneck, so protect it

The fleet's output is no longer limited by hours at a keyboard. It is limited by how fast you review PRs, answer gold markers, and make the calls only you can make.

That means the highest-leverage thing you do each day of this sprint is not building anything. It is the 20-minute morning merge window, the dials in Lane A, and quick decisive answers when a desk goes gold. The terminals convert your decisions into shipped work at a rate one person never could; starve them of decisions and they idle, feed them and the 11 days compound.

The shape of a good sprint day

Morning: dials + one merge window. Midday: glance at the wall, answer any gold. Evening: read the foreman report, dispatch tomorrow's intent from your phone. Everything else is the fleet's problem.
