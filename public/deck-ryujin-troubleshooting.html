05 / 12
How it works

The mental model

It works off the live code, not your computer

It always looks at the current Ryujin, so you never download or save anything.

You cannot break anything

Claude never touches the live site directly. It writes its fix into a branch, a safe copy. Nothing reaches customers until Mac reviews and approves it.

You drive with words

"The quote total looks too high on the proposal page, find out why" is a perfectly good instruction.

The simplest way to picture it

A very fast junior developer who shows their work, explains the cause, and never puts anything live without a sign-off.

The core loop

How a fix flows, start to finish

You only do the gold steps: notice, describe, review, verify. Claude does the code (teal). Mac approves what goes live (green).
07 / 12
The skill that matters most

Describe the problem, not the solution

Too vague

"The calendar is broken." "Fix the quotes." "It does not work."

Clear and specific

"When a customer books an inspection, they get two confirmation emails instead of one. Find why and fix it." "On the proposal page, the Diamond total shows before tax but the others show after tax. Make them consistent." "The Add Crew Member button on the team page does nothing when I click it."

The recipe

Say what you did, what you expected, and what actually happened. Paste the page name or link if you have it. The more specific you are, the better the fix.

08 / 12
Stay in your lane, for now

What to handle, what to hand up

✓
Go ahead
  • Reproduce and report bugs you find
  • Ask Claude to investigate and propose a fix
  • Small, clear fixes: wrong label, broken button, wrong number on a page
  • Ask Claude to read and explain how something works
⚠
Check with Mac first
  • Anything touching pricing, money, or a proposal already sent
  • Anything that emails or texts customers
  • Deleting data, or anything that says "database" or "migration"
  • A fix that changes a lot of files at once

When in doubt

Ask Claude: "Is this change risky, and who should approve it?" It will tell you straight. Then loop Mac in.

09 / 12
After you ask

What to look for in the answer

A plan, in plain English

Read it. If it matches the problem, say "go ahead." If not, correct it in your own words.

The cause

Claude should say why it broke, not only what it changed. If it does not, ask: "What was actually causing this?"

A branch or PR

This is the fix, packaged for Mac. Send him the link or the branch name so he can review and deploy.

A way to check

Ask: "How do I confirm this is fixed?" so you can verify it once it is live.

10 / 12
When it is urgent

If the live site is actually broken

If customers are hitting an error right now (a page is down, bookings are failing), that is an incident, not a routine fix.

  1. Tell Mac immediately. Do not wait to finish a full investigation first.
  2. In parallel, ask Claude Code: "The X page is throwing an error in production, what is the most likely cause?" Paste any error text you can see.
  3. Do not rush a fix to the live site yourself. Hand the diagnosis to Mac and let him drive the deploy.

Simple rule

Routine bug: you can drive. Live site down: Mac drives, you assist with the diagnosis.

11 / 12
Start to finish

A real one: the double booking email

You"When someone books an inspection, two confirmation emails go out. There should be one. Find why and fix it."
Claude"Two workflows are both sending a notice on a new booking. I will turn off the duplicate one and keep the main confirmation. Here is which, and why."
You"That matches what I see. Go ahead."
Claude"Done, saved on a branch. To confirm: book a test inspection and check that only one email arrives."

Then

Send Mac the branch, he reviews, merges, and deploys. You do the test booking and confirm one email. Fixed, and you never wrote a line of code. You described, reviewed, and verified.

Keep this handy

The whole thing on one card

  • Notice: something looks or behaves wrong
  • Describe: tell Claude what you did, expected, and saw
  • Review: read the plan, approve it or correct it
  • Hand up: send Mac the branch for anything live, pricing, or customer-facing
  • Verify: once it is live, confirm it is actually fixed
  • When stuck: ask Claude "explain this simply" or "is this risky, and who approves it?"
